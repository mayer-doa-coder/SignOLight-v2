# Project Vision

## What Is a Vision and Why Does It Matter?

Before you write a single line of code, before you pick a tool, before you think about what the avatar looks like — you need a vision. A vision is not a list of features. A vision is not a technical goal. A vision is a single, clear answer to the question: **"Why does this project exist in the world?"**

Think of it this way. If someone woke you up at 3 in the morning and asked "Why are you building this thing?", your vision should be the first words out of your mouth — not a list of bullet points about features, not a description of the technology, but a human reason that matters.

The best visions pass what you can call the **grandmother test**. If you can explain your project to a grandmother who has never used a computer, and she says, "Oh, that's important" — you have a good vision. If she looks confused, your vision is too technical.

Here is an example of a bad vision for this project:

> *"We are building an AI-powered multilingual speech-to-gloss NLP pipeline with a Three.js avatar renderer for accessibility compliance."*

Here is a good vision for the same project:

> *"We want to make sure that a deaf student in Bangladesh never has to miss what the teacher just said."*

Read both of those again. Notice how the second one makes you feel something. Notice how it is about a person and a moment, not about a technology. That is what a vision is.

## The Deeper Meaning of Your Project

Your project sits at the intersection of three things that are rarely combined: **education, language, and inclusion**. Let me explain each briefly.

Education is one of the most important forces in any person's life. When someone cannot access education equally, it changes every other part of their future — what jobs they can get, how much money they earn, whether they feel respected in society, and whether they can reach their potential.

Language is identity. For Deaf people, especially those who use sign language as their first language, sign language is not just a communication tool. It is culture. It is community. It is the way they think and feel and tell stories. A project that honours sign language is not just building a utility — it is showing respect for an entire community's way of being in the world.

Inclusion means belonging. Not "we tolerate you" but "we built this for you." There is a massive difference between a classroom where a deaf student sits in the back struggling to lipread and a classroom where every lecture is fully accessible in that student's language. Your project is trying to build a bridge between those two realities.

## What "Success" Looks Like in Human Terms

Here is a useful exercise. Imagine a specific student — let's call her Riya. Riya is 19 years old, studying computer science at a university in Dhaka. She has been Deaf since birth. Her sign language is Bangla Sign Language (BdSL). She sits in lectures where the professor speaks only in Bangla and English, writes on the board, and never faces the students. There is no interpreter. She is brilliant, but she is missing at least 60% of every lecture. She spends hours every night trying to reconstruct what she missed from textbook readings and help from classmates.

Now imagine Riya opens your application. The professor's voice is being transcribed. The captions appear in real time. A friendly animated avatar signs the key concepts beside the video. The language is simplified into clear sentences she can follow. If there's a word in the lecture she's never seen signed before — like "compiler" — the app shows her a simple explanation and attempts a sign for it.

At the end of class, Riya understood 90% of the lecture. She feels like she belongs. She does not go home exhausted from guessing. She does her homework confidently.

**That** is what success looks like. Not "the system achieved 94% gloss accuracy." Not "we have a latency of 350ms." Riya understood the lecture. That is the metric that matters.

## Common Beginner Mistake #1: Feature Confusion

Almost every first-time builder makes this mistake: they fall in love with features before they are in love with the vision.

You will find yourself saying things like, "We need real-time avatar animation" or "We should support 500 signs" or "We need a mobile version." Those are features. Features are means to an end. Your vision is the end.

The danger is this: when you build features without a clear vision, you will make decisions based on what is technically interesting rather than what actually serves Riya. You will spend two weeks perfecting facial expressions on the avatar when what Riya actually needs is reliable captions. You will add YouTube integration when what Riya actually needs is to understand her class lecture.

Every time you face a decision — "should we add this feature or not?" — the only useful question is: **"Does this make it more likely that Riya understands her lecture today?"**

---

## Common Beginner Mistake #2: Vision That Changes Weekly

Another very common mistake is a vision that shifts every time you talk to someone new. You show your project to a technical professor, and he says, "You should add speech emotion recognition," so now your vision includes emotion recognition. You talk to an investor who says, "This could be huge for enterprises," so now your vision is about corporate accessibility compliance.

A vision that bends to every suggestion is not a vision — it is a wish list. Real visions are stubborn. They change slowly, only when there is deep, honest evidence that the original direction was wrong.

Your vision for this project — making lecture content accessible to deaf students in Bangladesh through AI — is solid. Hold onto it. Write it on a piece of paper and pin it somewhere visible while you work.

---

## Risk: Losing the Human Focus

The biggest risk to your project vision is not technical failure. It is the gradual drift toward being a technology project instead of a human project. This happens quietly. You start caring more about the avatar looking smooth than about whether the captions are readable. You start measuring success by "how many signs are in our dictionary" rather than by "did we talk to any deaf students this week?"

Notice if your conversations with your team start focusing entirely on technical metrics. That is the warning sign. Redirect back to the human story.

---

## How to Validate Your Vision

A vision is validated through human contact, not through code. Before you build anything, go find two or three people who are Deaf or Hard-of-Hearing and who are currently studying. Ask them this one question: **"If a tool could help you understand lectures better, what would that look like?"**

Do not show them your project. Do not describe your features. Just listen. If what they describe resembles what you are building, your vision is on track. If they describe something completely different — that is the most valuable information you could possibly receive, and you should be grateful for it.

---

## How to Know This Phase Is Successful

You have completed the vision phase successfully when you can answer these questions without hesitation:

1. Can you explain this project in one sentence that a non-technical person would care about?
2. When you face a hard decision about features, does your vision give you a clear answer?
3. Have you spoken to at least one real potential user and confirmed that the problem you're solving is real to them?
4. Does every person on your team describe the project in roughly the same human terms, not just in technical terms?

If the answer to all four is yes, you are ready to move to the next phase.
