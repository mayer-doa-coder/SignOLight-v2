# Long-Term Vision

## Opening: From Student Project to Meaningful Contribution

What you are building for this hackathon is a proof of concept. It demonstrates that the core idea — a multilingual, AI-powered sign language assistant for academic lectures — is technically achievable, educationally valuable, and human-centred in its design. That is what a hackathon submission can be.

But the idea itself is much bigger than a hackathon submission. Done well, with sustained commitment and growing collaboration, this project could become something genuinely significant — for Bangladeshi Deaf education, for the documentation of Bangla Sign Language, for the global accessibility technology landscape, and for the millions of people in South Asia who use sign languages that existing technology completely ignores.

This section is about that larger possibility. It is a map of what this project could become — not as a fantasy, but as a realistic trajectory grounded in what you are already building and what the landscape of opportunity around it actually looks like.

## 20.1 Research Opportunities

### What This Means

The project you are building is not just a product — it is also a research platform. Every decision you make, every test you run, every user session you conduct generates data about a set of questions that the research community does not yet have good answers to. Framing your project as a research contribution alongside its practical contribution opens significant doors.

### Why Research Opportunities Matter

A project that produces a working application is impressive. A project that produces a working application *and* contributes to the scientific knowledge base is rare and far more impactful. Research contributions lead to published papers, which lead to academic recognition, collaborations with universities and research labs, access to funding sources, and the ability to attract talented contributors. All of these accelerate the project's development and increase its chance of long-term survival.

### The Research Questions Your Project Can Contribute To

**Question one: What are the specific linguistic structures of BdSL academic vocabulary?**

Your project is, in its current form, one of the first systematic attempts to create a digital BdSL vocabulary for academic content. The process of deciding what signs to use, validating them with community members, documenting them, and observing how they are used represents a genuine linguistic contribution. This data, if collected and documented carefully, could support a published paper in the field of sign language linguistics or language documentation.

**Question two: How does AI-generated gloss quality affect comprehension in Deaf learners?**

When your application generates gloss and an avatar performs it, does a Deaf student actually understand the content better than without the application? By how much? Under what conditions? These are empirical questions that could be tested through a structured study with a group of Deaf students. The results would contribute to the educational technology literature and to the sign language technology evaluation literature.

**Question three: What is the minimum viable sign dictionary size for meaningful educational support?**

Your application starts with 30–80 signs. Research on vocabulary coverage and comprehension in sign language contexts could help answer: at what dictionary size does the application become genuinely useful? At what size does it become excellent? This question has direct implications for prioritisation in sign language technology development.

**Question four: How does caption quality interact with sign quality in supporting Deaf learner comprehension?**

Your application presents both captions and signing simultaneously. Research on how these two channels interact — whether they are additive, redundant, or sometimes conflicting — would contribute to the design principles for multimodal accessibility systems.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Mistake one: Not collecting data systematically from the beginning.** Research contributions require data. If you start collecting data only after you decide to write a paper, you will have missed the most valuable early-stage observations. Start documenting your user sessions, testing findings, and design decisions from the beginning, with the thought that this documentation might eventually support a research publication.

**Mistake two: Underestimating the contribution of documentation work.** Linguists and accessibility researchers would be genuinely interested in a well-documented sign dictionary of 50 to 100 BdSL academic signs with validation notes. This might seem modest compared to a complex AI system, but it represents something that does not currently exist and could be cited and built upon by other researchers.

**Important areas to explore:** The proceedings of **LREC** (Language Resources and Evaluation Conference) contain many papers on building and evaluating language resources for under-documented languages, including sign languages. **Why they matter:** Understanding what constitutes a publishable research contribution in these communities allows you to design your work from the beginning to meet publication standards — collecting the right data, documenting methodology, and applying appropriate evaluation frameworks.

**What can realistically be reused:** Research methodology templates for user studies with Deaf participants, evaluation frameworks for sign language technology quality, and citation networks that connect your work to existing literature are all available in these sources.

## 20.2 Startup and Social Enterprise Opportunities

### What This Means

Beyond academic research, the system you are building has genuine commercial and social enterprise potential. A social enterprise is an organisation that applies business methods to social goals — it generates revenue to sustain itself, but its primary measure of success is social impact rather than profit. This model is often more appropriate than either a traditional startup or a nonprofit for projects that serve underserved communities.

### Why This Path Matters

A social enterprise model is relevant to this project for several reasons. The problem you are solving affects millions of people in South Asia, the vast majority of whom cannot pay a significant subscription fee for accessibility technology. A purely commercial model would either require premium pricing that excludes the most vulnerable users or depend on institutional buyers (universities, governments) that move slowly. A social enterprise model can generate revenue from institutional buyers — universities, hospitals, government agencies — while providing free or heavily subsidised access to individual students.

### The Revenue Landscape

There are several potential revenue sources that are compatible with a mission to serve Deaf students equitably.

**Institutional subscriptions.** Universities and polytechnics that adopt your application as part of their accessibility provision could pay a per-student or per-institution fee. This is the most scalable revenue model. As government pressure for accessible higher education grows globally — including in South Asia — the institutional market for accessibility tools will grow.

**Government partnerships.** The Bangladesh government has policies and programs related to education for students with disabilities. Several international development organisations (UN agencies, USAID, the UK's foreign development programs) have funding streams specifically for educational technology in developing countries. Grant funding from these sources, while not revenue, provides the capital needed to develop and deploy the application at scale.

**Corporate social responsibility programs.** Technology companies operating in Bangladesh increasingly have CSR commitments around digital inclusion and accessibility. Partnerships with these companies — providing sponsorship in exchange for recognition and involvement — can provide both funding and technical resources.

**Consulting and implementation services.** Universities that want to adopt the application may need help configuring it, training staff, and integrating it with existing systems. Providing these services at a fee generates revenue that subsidises free individual access.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Mistake one: Assuming the social mission and commercial success are incompatible.** Some of the most successful organisations in accessibility technology are social enterprises that have found ways to fund their mission through institutional revenue while maintaining free or low-cost access for individual users. This is not a contradiction; it is a sustainable model.

**Mistake two: Pursuing investment too early.** Seeking venture capital or angel investment before demonstrating real user adoption creates pressure to optimise for investor metrics rather than user outcomes. For a project at this stage, grants, competitions, and institutional partnerships are more appropriate funding sources than equity investment.

### Relevant Research Areas and Papers to Explore

**Research fields:** Social Entrepreneurship, ICT4D Business Models, Accessibility Technology Market

**Search keywords:**
- "social enterprise technology education developing countries"
- "assistive technology market accessibility"
- "ICT4D sustainable business models"
- "edtech social enterprise model"

**Important areas to explore:** The **Prahalad "Base of the Pyramid"** framework — about sustainable business models that serve low-income populations — is highly relevant. His book "The Fortune at the Bottom of the Pyramid" describes case studies of successful enterprises serving underserved populations. The business model principles he describes are applicable to your situation.

Research on **impact measurement for social enterprises** — how to demonstrate social outcomes to funders and institutional partners — is relevant for building the evidence base you would need to attract institutional buyers.

**Why they matter:** Understanding which revenue models are compatible with a mission to serve Deaf students equitably allows you to plan for sustainability without compromising the project's core purpose.

**What can realistically be reused:** The impact measurement frameworks from social enterprise research can be adapted to define the specific metrics you would use to demonstrate the value of your application to institutional buyers and grant funders.

## 20.3 Government and University Adoption

### What This Means

One of the most impactful possible outcomes for this project is institutional adoption — its integration into the standard accessibility provisions of universities across Bangladesh, or its endorsement and funding by government bodies responsible for disability rights and education.

### The Government Pathway

Bangladesh has commitments under the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which includes specific provisions about equal access to education. The Ministry of Social Welfare and the Ministry of Education have roles in implementing these commitments. A tool that demonstrably improves the educational experience of Deaf students, developed locally, in Bengali, using BdSL — this is exactly the kind of solution that government bodies responsible for CRPD implementation should be interested in.

The pathway to government engagement begins with documentation. Building an evidence base — user studies, accessibility audits, comparative analyses, expert endorsements — that demonstrates the application's impact in language that policy makers understand is the foundation. Government decisions are made on evidence of impact, not on technical sophistication.

### The University Adoption Pathway

Universities that have begun thinking about accessibility provisions — typically starting with the larger institutions in Dhaka — represent a more immediate adoption opportunity. The pathway typically starts with a disability support office or an individual faculty member who champions the tool, moves to a pilot program with a small group of Deaf students, and then to a formal institutional adoption decision.

What universities need to see before adopting: evidence of effectiveness, reassurance about reliability, clarity about cost, and confidence that the tool will be maintained. Your work on production readiness, user validation, and long-term sustainability directly addresses these requirements.

### Relevant Research Areas and Papers to Explore

**Research fields:** Educational Policy, Disability Rights in Higher Education, Technology Adoption in Developing Country Universities

**Search keywords:**
- "CRPD implementation higher education developing countries"
- "disability policy Bangladesh university"
- "accessibility technology institutional adoption"
- "edtech government adoption framework"

**Important areas to explore:** The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) text and its Optional Protocol are freely available at the UN website. Article 24, specifically on education, is directly relevant. Reading this article gives you the legal and rights framework within which your project operates and can be advocated for.

Research on **technology adoption in public institutions** — specifically in developing country higher education — reveals the specific barriers and enablers that affect whether a technology gets officially adopted or remains informal. This knowledge allows you to design your approach to institutional engagement strategically.

**Why they matter:** Understanding the institutional adoption process allows you to build evidence and relationships in advance of formal adoption conversations, rather than being caught unprepared when a promising opportunity arises.

## 20.4 Future AI Possibilities

### What This Means

The AI capabilities available today are limited in ways that matter for your project — limited transcription accuracy for Bengali academic speech, limited sign language generation quality, no ability to truly learn a new sign language from scratch. But AI capabilities are advancing rapidly, and the landscape will look different in three to five years in ways that are relevant to your project.

### Near-Term AI Developments That Will Affect Your Project

**Improved multilingual speech recognition.** Models specifically designed for low-resource languages, including Bengali, are improving consistently. Research programs at Google, Meta, and academic institutions are producing models that perform better on regional accents, code-switching, and domain-specific vocabulary. As these models become available, your transcription quality will improve without requiring changes to your own system.

**Generative sign language avatars.** Research into generating sign language from text using neural networks — rather than playing pre-animated clips — is active and advancing. Systems that can generate novel sign language sequences for words not in a pre-built dictionary would eliminate the most significant limitation of your current approach. Within three to five years, this capability may be good enough to meaningfully extend your system's coverage. Watching this research area and being ready to integrate advances as they become practical is part of your long-term technical strategy.

**Large language models and educational simplification.** The models available for simplifying academic language are already powerful and are improving continuously. Your simplification step, which currently achieves good but imperfect results, will improve as the underlying models improve. Staying current with advances in educational text simplification — particularly research focused on Bengali — is relevant.

### Relevant Research Areas and Papers to Explore

**Research fields:** Neural Sign Language Generation, Low-Resource ASR, Educational AI, Accessible AI

**Search keywords:**
- "neural sign language generation diffusion models"
- "sign language production generative AI"
- "low resource ASR Bengali multilingual"
- "educational text simplification large language models"
- "accessible AI future directions"

**Important areas to explore:** Research at the intersection of **motion synthesis and sign language** — using diffusion models, transformers, and other generative approaches to produce novel sign language animations from text — represents the frontier that will eventually replace pre-animated sign dictionaries. Papers from 2023 and 2024 in this area give you a sense of how close this frontier is to practical applicability.

**Why they matter:** Understanding the near-term AI research trajectory allows you to design your system now in ways that will be easy to extend as better AI components become available. Systems designed to be modular — where an improved component can be swapped in — benefit more from AI progress than monolithic systems.

**What can realistically be reused:** The evaluation frameworks from sign language generation research give you benchmarks against which to compare your current system and targets toward which to plan future improvements.

## 20.5 Bangla Sign Language Advancement

### What This Means

One of the most significant long-term contributions your project could make is not to accessibility technology but to the documentation, formalization, and advancement of Bangla Sign Language itself.

BdSL is currently under-documented. There is no comprehensive BdSL dictionary in digital form. There is no large corpus of BdSL video data that could be used to train AI systems. There is no standardised system for writing BdSL down. For a language used by millions of people, this is a significant gap — one that perpetuates the marginalisation of BdSL speakers in education, media, and public life.

Your project, if extended with appropriate community involvement and academic rigour, could contribute to closing this gap.

### How Your Project Contributes to BdSL

Every sign in your dictionary that has been validated by BdSL community members is a small contribution to BdSL documentation. Every gloss sequence that has been reviewed for grammatical accuracy is a small contribution to BdSL linguistic knowledge. The aggregate of these contributions, over time, could form the foundation of a comprehensive academic BdSL resource.

This contribution has effects beyond your application. Researchers studying BdSL can use it. Sign language interpreters training in BdSL can reference it. Deaf schools creating educational materials can draw on it. The TV stations and media organisations that want to add BdSL content can use it.

### Relevant Research Areas and Papers to Explore

**Research fields:** Language Documentation, Endangered Language Preservation, Sign Language Corpus Linguistics, BdSL Research

**Search keywords:**
- "sign language corpus documentation methodology"
- "endangered language digital preservation"
- "BdSL Bangladesh sign language documentation"
- "sign language standardisation community"

**Important areas to explore:** The **Endangered Languages Project** (endangeredlanguages.com) and its associated research community have developed methodology for documenting under-resourced languages that is applicable to sign language documentation. Their ethical guidelines for working with language communities are particularly relevant.

Research from **Gallaudet University's linguistics department** on signed language corpus building provides direct methodology for the type of BdSL documentation work your project could contribute to.

**Why they matter:** Framing your sign dictionary work as a contribution to BdSL documentation — rather than just a component of an app — positions it as something with value beyond your specific application and increases its research and community impact.

**What can realistically be reused:** The documentation standards and metadata schemas from language documentation research can be applied to how you record, describe, and store each sign in your dictionary, making it more useful to future researchers and community members.

## 20.6 International Expansion and the Broader Sign Language Technology Ecosystem

### What This Means

While your immediate focus is Bangladesh and BdSL, the methodology, architecture, and lessons learned from building this application are applicable to sign languages across South Asia and beyond. Indian Sign Language (ISL), Nepali Sign Language, Sri Lankan Sign Language — all of these are under-served by existing technology, all are used by large communities of Deaf signers, and all share many of the challenges you have encountered with BdSL.

### Why the Regional Opportunity Matters

The total number of Deaf people in South Asia is estimated in the tens of millions. The existing sign language technology infrastructure for this region is essentially zero. A project that demonstrates a successful model for one South Asian sign language — and then publishes its methodology, makes its platform extensible, and builds partnerships with Deaf communities in neighbouring countries — could become the foundation of an entire regional accessibility infrastructure.

This is not a small ambition. But it is a grounded one, rooted in the real gap between the need and the existing supply of sign language technology in the region.

### The Ecosystem Role

Beyond your own application, you have the opportunity to contribute to the broader ecosystem of sign language technology in ways that benefit other builders and researchers. By open-sourcing your platform, publishing your methodology, documenting your sign dictionary, and sharing your lessons learned, you make it easier for others to build on what you have done rather than starting from scratch.

This ecosystem-building role — being not just a product but a platform and a knowledge base — is what distinguishes projects that create lasting impact from projects that create isolated impact.

### Relevant Research Areas and Papers to Explore

**Research fields:** Regional Accessibility Ecosystems, South Asian Sign Languages, Open Source Accessibility Technology, Technology Ecosystem Development

**Search keywords:**

**Why they matter:** Understanding the sign language technology landscape across South Asia helps you identify potential partners, avoid duplicating work already done by others in the region, and position your project's contribution in a regional context that multiplies its impact.

**What can realistically be reused:** The open-source licensing frameworks, community contribution models, and internationalisation patterns from existing accessibility technology projects can be adapted to make your platform extensible for other South Asian sign languages.

## 20.7 Accessibility Ecosystem Impact

### What This Means

The longest-range vision for your project is not the application itself but the ecosystem it creates — the community of developers, researchers, linguists, Deaf advocates, educators, and policy makers who are drawn together around the shared goal of making education accessible for Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing students in Bangladesh and beyond.

Ecosystems are more resilient and more impactful than individual products. A single application, however well built, can be abandoned when the team moves on. An ecosystem — a community of practice with shared knowledge, shared resources, and shared commitment — persists and grows.

Building an ecosystem is a long-term project. But it begins with small acts that create openness: open-sourcing your code, sharing your methodology, publishing your findings, making your sign dictionary available to others, inviting collaboration rather than protecting your work. Each of these acts contributes to an ecosystem that extends far beyond what any single team could build alone.

### Why Ecosystem Thinking Matters Now

Even at the hackathon stage, thinking about ecosystem impact shapes decisions that matter. Choosing to open-source your code, choosing to document your sign dictionary publicly, choosing to publish your methodology — these decisions cost nothing at this stage and lay the foundation for the ecosystem contributions that will matter in the long run.

The accessibility technology ecosystem in South Asia is not yet built. You have an early-mover opportunity to shape it — not just through your application but through the norms, practices, and shared resources you contribute to building.
